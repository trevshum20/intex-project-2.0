Bad News and Good News



This graph shows our predicted amount of deaths due to opioids through 2024. Using data from the last 15 years from the CDC1, we have predicted that Opioid related deaths will increase in the next three years if measures are not taken to change the current status quo.

One good sign is that the amount of opioids being prescribed have fallen harshly in the last decade, meaning that the medical field is becoming increasingly more responsible with prescribing opioids. However, deaths are still increasing, likely due to the fact that many street drugs are opioids and certain street drugs like fentanyl are becoming more common.



Street Drugs Abound


Fentanyl is an opioid that is 10,000 times more potent than morphine. Up until the last decade it was mostly only ever seen in hospitals. Recently, fentanyl has been manufactured and sold illegally, making it the leading cause of death among all opioids, and being responsible for 73% of opioid related deaths. Fentanyl use is 11 times higher than it was in 2006.2

The above graph shows that fentanyl use has increased drastically all across the United States from 2018 - 2019.

According to the CDC, the source of this fentanyl is "more likely to be illicitly manufactured than pharmaceutical".3,4,5 This further proves that the medical field is becoming more responsible and causing less opioid deaths, while illegal drug trade is increasing opioid deaths at an alarming rate.


Worrisome Proportions



For all of the accidental deaths in each state, the above graph shows which percent of accidental deaths were caused by opioids (CDC)1. Utah attributes the highest percent of their accidental deaths to opioids compared to all other states.

Despite having smaller populations, some states have a disproportionately higher percent of their citizens dying from opioids.
